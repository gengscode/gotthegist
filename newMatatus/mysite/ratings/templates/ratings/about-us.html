The Need

The problems we are addressing

Safety

Matatu drivers often disobey basic traffic regulations by speeding, driving on sidewalks, going into oncoming traffic, or driving drunk. Reckless matatus contribute to Kenya having the 15th highest rate of road fatalities per motor vehicle in the world.

Quality

Some Matatu drivers are safe and courteous, while others are not. However, customers do not know which ones are good and which ones are not. Plus, a customer does not know when the next “good” Matatu is going to arrive, so he/she ends up getting on the “bad” Matatu instead of waiting and risking that the “good” one does not come for hours.

Schedules

Matatus do not run on schedules and traffic makes the frequencies of arrivals highly irregular. So, it is impossible to know where matatus are and when they’ll arrive.

Traffic

Nairobi consistently ranks among the world’s top ten worst cities for traffic. At the end of a long workday, accurate traffic reports would help residents decide whether or not they should leave their offices immediately or wait until traffic clears.

Navigation

Until the Digital Matatus project mapped the full system last year, there had never been a single map of the entire bus system. Even with the new map, there are literally hundreds of stops and almost 150 routes. It is still very difficult to navigate the system.

Maintenance/Costs

Matatu owners do not want their drivers to drive recklessly, as going on sidewalks or swerving into oncoming traffic increases wear-and-tear and the risk of accidents. The insurance companies also want to know if the vehicles they are covering are driven in a safe manner or not, so that they can adjust the rates accordingly.

The Solution

How Smart Matatus improves the Nairobi transporation system

Rating System

The system will allow customers to identify the good drivers, punish the bad drivers (through loss of business), and serve as a mechanism to encourage safe driving.

Directions

Customers could enter their starting and end points, we’d tell them the fastest and easiest matatu route(s) to take- just like Google Maps does for US public transport.

GPS Tracking System

We’d put a tracker in every Matatu to locate them 24/7 and estimate arrival times. The hundreds of trackers would also help estimate traffic.

Scale Up Plan

How we will roll out the project in phases

Phase #1: Rating System (MPV)

The easiest part of the system to implement is the quality rating system (basically, Yelp for matatus). This step does not require the cooperation from anybody other than the customers. I already set up a basic, functional version at smartmatatus.com, using the list of matatu routes from the Digital Matatus project. I do not have the license plate numbers of the matatus, so I need customers to start using the platform and organically adding/rating them. Over time, every matatu will be rated and we will compile a database of every matatu in Nairobi. To advertise, I’d start a publicity campaign, using my contacts at Nairobi news agencies, paying for ads, and hiring boots on the ground to spread the word at different matatu stops.

Phase #2: Directions

Using data from the Digital Matatus project, I already added the GPS coordinates for every Matatu stop in Nairobi to Google Maps (click the Directions link on the site). With a few more weeks of coding, I could overlay this information onto Google Maps to give customers directions after they enter their starting and end points.

Phase #3: GPS Tracking System

The most critical and challenging part of the project is getting GPS trackers in each Matatu. The path of least resistance would be through the insurance companies and owners, who both want to track the drivers to identify bad behavior. If the owners/insurance companies simply mandated that their drivers allow GPS trackers in their vehicles, then we could get decent uptake.

Another option would be approaching government to get them to mandate GPS trackers, as there is a precedent for government intervention in the Matatu space. John Michuki, the former Minister for Transport and Communications, instituted the “Michuki Rules” in the early 2000’s. The rules required all matatus to install speed governors, passenger safety belts, operate in clearly defined routes, and to carry a specified number of passengers. Since Mr. Michuki’s death in 2012, these rules have not been enforced, but with proper lobbying, we could get government to intervene again on our behalf.

In the long run, however, we’d prefer to get buy in from the drivers themselves and not just enforcement from above. To do so, I’d start by approaching the “good” Matatu drivers who want to differentiate themselves based on quality. If the GPS trackers lead to increase demand for these quality matatus, then other Matatu drivers might see their value and follow suit.

NGOs/Universities/Foundations

Already Georgetown University, USAID, MIT, the Center for Sustainable Urban Development (CSUD), and the University of Nairobi, have all backed projects related to improving the Nairobi’s matatu system. We could likely raise money from them to start the project.

Matatu Owners + Insurance Companies

As stated, matatu owners and insurance companies want to track the matatus, so might be willing to pay for the purchase and installation of the GPS trackers.

Users (Questionable)

As the project is designed for public benefit, I would be very hesitant to charge customers for using the site. In the long run, however, we might offer users the ability to pay to reserve bus seats in advance and the site could take a small fraction of those sales.

Challenges/Concerns

The greatest risks to the project's success

Driver Resistance

Drivers generally do not want their owners to know how recklessly they are driving or how much money they are really making. So, many would resist GPS trackers.

Available Seating Information

From talking with Adam White at Digital Matatus, I learned that another big need for customers is knowing not only when the next matatu is arriving, but if it has available seats. It’d be hard to get that info without the driver’s help. Unfortunately, drivers are incentivized to lie to underreport their revenue to the owners. A potential solution would be to allow customers to pay extra to pre-book seats on the matatu and then drivers would be incentivized to be honest about how many seats are available.

Gangs

Criminal gangs run some of the matatu routes. They might attack users who give them negative ratings. For this reason, we’d keep the ratings anonymous.
